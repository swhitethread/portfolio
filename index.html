SAMANTHA WHITETHREAD





I am a second year Design student at Goldsmiths University. I have a wide and varied skillset, and pride myself on my ability to adapt to meet a projects needs and requirements. I enjoy speculations, often through materials, and believe that a problem is best solved through making. My practice is situated within the realm of crafts, the hand made, and the do it yourself territory. CRAFTING A COMMUNITY

USING CRAFT TO BREAKDOWN SOCIAL BARRIERS, CREATING RELATIONSHIPS AND ALLOWING INDIVIDUALS TO EXPRESS THEIR STORIES BOTH VERBALLY AND ARTISTICALLY.


Previous Next

A project commissioned by The Innovation Unit for Goldsmiths University with the aim to create ‘Hope’ in the London borough of Lambeth. As a non-profit organisation that focuses on public and social issues, they aim to treat the symptoms of deep rooted problems by changing structures and working closely with local charities. One particular way that they do this is by engaging with people and their individual stories and experiences to create a personal solution to a problem.

Very quickly we began our mission of defining ‘Hope’. To us it was about positivity and celebration, we set about trying to meet with as many people individuals around Lambeth as possible. Making observations about the location, it was important that we thought about setting scenes and proposing ways for people to engage with the spaces that they inhabited in a positive way. After visiting Brixton on several occasions we settled on using the place as a kind of formula that could be used and spread to a wider community. We had real admiration for the culture and community which is constantly under threat by acts of gentrification. The vibrancy and colours of the place are infectious and inspired creativity within us, it became something that we endeavoured to reflect in our work.

We devised an activity that would provide the opportunity to engage with Brixton residents in a way that was neither invasive nor patronising – ‘People of Lambeth’. We asked people to draw something that represented Lambeth to them. Residents were given a sheet of paper and complete creative freedom, some residents chose to draw themselves, feeling that the most important aspect of Lambeth to them was the people in it. What amassed from this was the start of an archive that would be used to map people and their stories through a visual medium.

Concentrating on the history of Brixton and particularly its residents, we used reggae reggae sauce created by Levi Roots, to generate a series of images based on the notion of exquisite corpse which went alongside a series of drawings made using the map lines of our routes around Brixton.

Previous Next
Taking these small details only highlighted objects around our location that are overlooked, using manhole covers a series of prints were devised – celebrating and utilising the mundane.

These prints lead us to the definitive location of the project – Coldharbour Lane. It is a place of great conflict with 22 shootings taking place between 2011 and 2013 alone. Over the course of the last 10 years the shops and businesses that could be found there have changed drastically with the last black pub in Brixton – ‘The Angel’ – closing down to make way for ‘Mamma Dough’, a pizza parlour. Also along the road is a delightful café called ‘CAYA’, ran by Kate (featured in ‘People of Lambeth’) which although follows in the footsteps of other gentrified businesses, provides a workspace for local residents.

Through CAYA and speaking to Kate we discovered that originally she disliked the building that her shop is situated opposite - Southwyck House, but grew to love it. She described it as looking like a prison, but from its iconic architecture which we greatly admired, our energies became focused on changing opinions on Southwyck House and exploring its possibilities.

Previous Next
We began carrying our investigations into the block, exploring within the estate and engaging with residents and trying to gain access to the elusive house which proved a real struggle. We felt that it was important to do so in order to gain the same perspective as the occupants, and from talking to people we began to build a wider picture of peoples views of the estate, a surprisingly varied range of opinions. From this we unearthed the myths and legends surrounding the space and initially planned a narrative to create a draw to the building and its stories. However, we found that our ‘rapid response’ style of quick making made for a very hands on approach and the notions of craft that had been persistent throughout the project began to become prevalent.

We took the iconic Barrier Block shape and ran with it as a tool to propel us forward. Making prints and embroidery with Brixton colours, we started to think even more about how we could directly incorporate Southwyck House into this process. Whilst walking round the block we looked for materials that could be repurposed and upcycled to be used for craft. In doing so we found a fuse box and a bag of copper wire, we started to experiment with the materials at hand, and jewellery seemed to be the best fit.

We wanted to share this process of making with others and looked to find a space to create and collaborate. Opposite the block is Brixton Soup Kitchen, this we found was a great fit as it to had a connection with Southwyck House. We were welcomed in to the kitchen and we believed that our crafts could be of benefit to the individuals and create a positive atmosphere within. We tested the space and had a really great response, being invited back to get to know people and begin to form the foundations of relationships with them. Using the wire that we sourced, fabrics, and upcycled plastic sheets from Southwyck House skip, we created a template that enabled people to craft and simultaneously open a dialogue, sharing stories and experiences. We initially went with a previously devised activity where we had the intention for people to draw/stitch onto some collaborative sheets, however people began asking for specific skills (such as learning to crochet) and the ‘Crafternoon’ became much more about skill sharing from both sides of the table. This became a real positive element to the workshop as it created entirely unexpected the results and opened up the conversation between different groups of people as it provided a common ground, the materials were an equaliser.

We also managed to build a relationship with the founder of the Soup kitchen, Solomon Smith, who had a similar outlook towards the barrier block as us, as he grew up and still lives in the estate - It came in a full circle.

A RAPID RESPONSE TO THE COURGETTE CRISIS

Previous Next

In 2017 we experienced a crisis that was set to cause mass panic and unimaginable outrage - the courgette crisis. Due to adverse weather conditions, supermarkets were unable to stock our favourite vegetable. Fortunately, a team of experts (The Courgetteers) took matters into their own hands and devised a machine that was capable of converting gold into courgettes (a far more coveted luxury). The very last courgette in recorded history was cryogenically frozen for historical records. Now 100 years later, we re-animated the specimen for our fusion testing. Drawing out the DNA and analysing the structure we were able to digitally model the courgette and use specific protein coding sequences, splicing DNA from objects in our historical archive. The core-jet systematically analyses the effects of DNA variants on the main steps of gene regulation, from the chromatin state through protein function, to create the perfect courgette.

Unveiled for the first time at the Goldsmiths Pataphysical conference, the CORE-JET 2000 is an exceptional machine that works through the outlined delicate procedure of infusing gold with bio-vegmassness to produce outstanding results.

This was created as a rapid response to the ‘Courgette Crisis’, over the course of 5 days the matter was investigated and responded to through quick making and rapid prototyping. This method in particular was key in the production of this exploration as the speed at which it moved proved a key tool in identifying topic themes and responding to them in a manner that made the most of the time frame and enabled a large archive of material to be gathered and made. This method of rapid response has been a technique for production which I often engage with for both idea and material generation, often finding that it allows for a team to make the most of their time and accomplish a great amount.

Focussing on the process of ‘Alchemy’ - turning one object into something else – we repurposed this term by attaching it to a modern day issue and day 1 involved collecting information on this crisis in order to build a solid archive to work from. Day 2 progressed into focussing on the notion of mass production and the many different versions of the ‘courgettes’ came into fruition. This process of ‘anything can be become anything’ became a pivotal idea behind the creation of these objects and the uniqueness of each courgette aided our narrative of a machine that was attempting to do the impossible. Day 3 was the branding of ‘The Courgetteers’ and our mission, a logo was devised and badges created. We also used this to promote our cause, creating quick videos such as news reports (featured below) and from internet archival material that could be used within the conference. The Machine was coded and the process of courgette creation started to become a real possibility – we were getting closer to the real thing. Day 4 included the completion of the machine, with dials added to control factors such as gold quality and courgette size. With a brand with were now able to make a machine that embodied our mission both aesthetically and practically. Day 5 was were it all game to a close, with great pleasure I can announce that The Courgetteers succeeded in their mission to highlight themes of both mass production and mass consumerism in the making of the Core-Jet 2000.

ESCAPE THROUGH CAMOUFLAGE

This is an exploration into the use of camouflage as an escape. It is a methodology that has been devised in order to focus on using space as a platform to build a narrative through a performative medium. It is an exploration into using invisibility in one system to highlight and give visibility to another. Through doing this I was questioning what it means to take on the identity of a space, to play with its authority and question its boundaries.

The theatrics created when engaging with a space such as a tube station bring an element of a power play, as there is the transition between a visible feature and an ‘invisible’ camouflage. The location really allowed me to engage with this method of expanding a moment through costume. There was also a great deal of improvisation with this piece, using things such as old sheets and clothes to make the costumes. This DIY homemade aesthetic emphasises the notion that anyone can be a part of this small act of ‘protest’. Through hiding your identity and embodying that of our surroundings we can take action against types of tracking such as social media and CCTV.

This is creating the contrast to our 15 minutes of fame, it is our 15 minutes of invisibility.

NOWISM

“Disdain the example and admonition of tradition in order to invent at any cost something new which everyone considers crazy” (The Futurist Cookbook, 1989)


Previous Next

Nowism is an ideology created as an answer to human kinds dependence on and obsession with time. It is a solution to routine and conformity, it is against the image and the icon of the clock. Nowists believe that the clock is a tool designed to control the masses and dictate the structures of society. Its usage deviates from core human abilities; it intervenes with ‘gut feeling’ and applies pressure to those who aren’t ticking along with it.

This project explores these notions and through the creation of ‘Time Trolling’, the idea of disrupting time keeping methods and creating a disconnect within the system.

An example of a Time tradition is the way that the clocks get turned back an hour every Autumn and forward every Spring - ‘Daylight Saving Time’. The hundredth anniversary of this event occurred on the 30th October 2016, where at 2am all clocks reverted back to Greenwich meantime. The idea behind it is that it would reduce fuel consumption and increase productivity. Nowism argues that this method of increased productivity is one that we do not benefit from, it is a method of control and of compliance. As an act of protest, the art of ‘Time Trolling’ has been devised in order to fight back against the clock. Time Trolling is a Nowist action to disrupt the conventional methods of time keeping in any way that a Nowist may see fit. In doing so we create a disconnect, breaking the reliance on the clock and disrupting the Anti – Nowists. These actions aim to create a sense of freedom and liberation. Here I present a collection of Time Trolling methods to be used and manipulated in a variety of situations and places. A list of locations that have been visited and disrupted by Nowists with success are as followed:

- Kings Cross St Pancras (We encourage the disruption of all public transport)
- Big Ben, Westminster
- Greenwich Observatory and Meantime Line
- Canada Square, Canary Wharf
- Goldsmiths University of London

Time Trolling can be done using various methods, for example using sound to drown out the noise of a train announcement or clock chime. The sound can be words or phrases that you have devised, recording a chant of “NOW NOW NOW” to play was often effective. Another way to do it is to use a speaker and microphone, this allows for more of a unique adaption of the announcement technique. Another option is to use visual methods to physically disrupt a person’s ability to use the clock. Duct tape is a highly recommended material for this, taping over a clock is a simple and effective way of protesting against the construction of time. In order to protest effectively, there are four simple rules created by Nowists to ensure for maximum success.

1. Dress the part – These placards baring multiple Nowists slogans such as “Stop The Clock” make for a clear and concise method of ensuring that a protest is a strong statement of defiance.
2. Amplify your Nowist Voice – Time Trolling is key here, making chants and shouting of slogans is encouraged.
3. Argue your point against the Anti Nowists – Questions are good, explaining what we are about and stimulating conversation is very important in the recruitment process. There have been various occasions where we have been asked to leave by a person of authority, do not let this deter you there are still plenty of other locations to disrupt.
4. Be The Now - Follow your gut, disrupt the clock, be the solution.

JEWELLERY DESIGN

This is an exploration into the field of jewellery design and the process of engaging with an outsourced fabricator to bring a piece of work to life.

These are the technical drawings and prototypes for a set of six rings that were designed to be cast in silver. There are two different shapes, three circular faces and three square, with the band remaining the same.

This highlighted to me the importance of model making and physicality when outsourcing an idea, in making sure that my idea is translated as quickly and as easily as possible. It also gave me a greater idea of the type of companies that are available to contact for such a need, broadening my knowledge of the industry.

BATOPHOBIA

Batophobia is the specific fear of high objects, such as tall buildings. Batophobia may be related to acrophobia — the fear of high places, or to anablepophobia — the fear of looking up at high places.

This project is an explorationinto the fear of falling from a great height by staking a bet and losing. The financial aspect of this plays out as a platform for exploring a different kind of highs and lows. The stock market has great wins and great loses, but is often inaccessible to the majority. Something like horse racing is an easy bet, and brings the rush of a win/lose situation to all. The recent rise in companies that provide platforms for gambling online and the high street areexplored and visited within this project.
